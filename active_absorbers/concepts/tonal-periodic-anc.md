# Tonal and Periodic ANC

**Scaffold page — sources pending.** Dedicated home for the special
case where the disturbance is (nearly) a single sinusoid or a discrete
harmonic comb locked to a rotating-machinery shaft. Broadband [[fxlms-algorithm]]
is overkill here; the narrowband case admits dedicated controllers that
converge faster, use fewer taps, and are much easier to stabilize.

Applies to cooling-fan blade-passing-frequency (BPF) hum, compressor
whine, pump-vane tones, transformer hum, and any other disturbance whose
spectrum is a line — the motivating application is **data-center
rooftop fan hum near 118 Hz** (4-blade fan at ~1770 RPM, or similar).

## 1. Why narrowband deserves its own treatment

| Property | Broadband FxLMS | Narrowband / tonal |
|---|---|---|
| Reference | upstream mic | $\sin/\cos$ pair at known $f$ (tacho) |
| Filter length | $L \sim 100$–$1000$ taps | **2 taps** (amplitude + phase) |
| Causality burden | strict | essentially absent (periodic ⇒ any phase offset is valid) |
| Convergence | slow; depends on $S(z)$ whitening | fast; two-weight gradient |
| Secondary-path sensitivity | phase error $< 90°$ over *whole band* | phase error $< 90°$ only at $f_0$ |
| Stability | delicate | easy |

A 118 Hz tonal is effectively a 2-dimensional estimation problem
(in-phase and quadrature). Everything below exploits that.

## 2. Synchronous / tacho-referenced ANC

The single highest-leverage technique for rotating machinery.

- A **shaft tachometer** (Hall sensor, optical encoder, or even a
  microphone plus PLL locked to the BPF line) provides a clean
  phase reference $\theta(n)$ locked to the fan rotation.
- Reference signals are synthesized digitally:
  $x_c(n) = \cos(\theta(n)), \; x_s(n) = \sin(\theta(n))$.
- Two FxLMS weights $w_c, w_s$ adapt the anti-noise amplitude/phase.
- Works at any fan RPM, including during run-up / run-down —
  the reference tracks the instantaneous $f_0(n)$ automatically.
- Extends trivially to harmonics: one $(\cos, \sin)$ pair per harmonic
  $k f_0, \; k = 1, 2, \ldots, K$ with independent $(w_{c,k}, w_{s,k})$.

## 3. Single-frequency FxLMS (FxLMS-SF) and adaptive notch

When no tacho is available, estimate $f_0$ from the residual spectrum
and drive a two-tap filter against synthesized $\cos/\sin$ at that
frequency.

- **Widrow sinusoidal-reference canceller** — Widrow 1975 already
  treats this case ([[lms-algorithm]], §on periodic interference).
- **Adaptive notch filter (ANF)** — constrained IIR with a notch at
  $f_0$; $f_0$ itself adapts via a gradient on the residual.
- **FxLMS-SF** — the filtered-x correction is applied only at $f_0$;
  $\hat{S}(e^{j2\pi f_0})$ is a single complex gain, trivial to
  estimate.
- Suffers if $f_0$ drifts faster than the estimator bandwidth (e.g.
  VFD-driven fan changing RPM mid-adaptation) — hence tacho (§2) is
  preferred when available.

## 4. Repetitive control (RC) and iterative learning control (ILC)

For strictly periodic disturbances, classical control theory offers
*plug-in* controllers that provide infinite loop gain at $f_0$ and its
harmonics — far stronger than any FxLMS can approach in steady state.

- **Internal-Model Principle** — Francis & Wonham: to reject a signal
  generated by an autonomous model $M(z)$, embed $M^{-1}(z)$ in the
  loop. For periodic disturbance of period $N$, $M(z) = 1/(1 - z^{-N})$.
- **Repetitive controller** — $C_{\text{RC}}(z) = Q(z) z^{-N}/(1 - Q(z) z^{-N})$
  with a low-pass $Q(z)$ to restrict the infinite-gain comb to
  frequencies where the plant is well-modelled.
- **ILC** — trial-domain analogue: the control signal for period
  $k{+}1$ is the period-$k$ signal plus a filtered residual. Offline /
  batch-mode.
- [[classical-anc-overview]] §8 cites 120 dB exhaust-noise reduction
  with RC — more than any FxLMS result in that table.
- Trade-offs: RC assumes *strict* periodicity; fan-speed drift breaks
  the comb. Hybrid RC + adaptive BPF-tracking addresses this.

## 5. Harmonic ANC (BPF + $k$·BPF)

Community-noise complaints about fan hum often track the second and
third harmonics (236 Hz, 354 Hz for a 118 Hz BPF) more than the
fundamental — they punch through A-weighting and overlap with speech.
Neglecting them makes the residual subjectively worse than raw.

- Synchronous-reference ANC (§2) handles this natively: add
  $(\cos, \sin)$ pairs at $k f_0$.
- Weight the cost per harmonic using a **psychoacoustic loudness
  weight** — see [[psychoacoustic-anc]].
- Beware of inter-harmonic cross-coupling through a nonlinear secondary
  path (speaker overdrive at high SPL) — Volterra / FLANN extensions
  from [[classical-anc-overview]] §4 may be needed.

## 6. AI-era extensions worth flagging

- **Meta-learning for tonal** — Feng & So 2024 ([[meta-learning-anc]])
  is demonstrated on broadband, but the attention-RNN gradient
  predictor would speed up tonal convergence by initialising $(w_c, w_s)$
  near the converged pose for similar RPMs.
- **RL for tonal** — overkill for a clean $(w_c, w_s)$ problem, but
  could handle harmonic weighting and loudspeaker saturation jointly
  (see [[deep-rl-anc]]).
- **Selective ANC** — pre-tuned $(w_c, w_s)$ banks indexed by fan RPM;
  natural extension of SFANC / GFANC (see [[classical-anc-overview]] §7).

## 7. Design notes for data-center rooftop fans

- Multiple fans → multiple independent BPFs (slight RPM drift between
  units). Either (a) one tacho + synchronous reference **per fan**,
  superposed, or (b) one wideband reference mic plus harmonic bank.
- Ground reflection and downwind propagation (ISO 9613) mean the
  quiet zone of interest is tens of metres away, not at an accessible
  error mic — motivates virtual sensing ([[pinn-virtual-sensing]]).
- Intermittent VFD modulation produces sidebands around $f_0$; the
  tonal controller must handle $(f_0 \pm f_m)$ or spectrum leakage
  defeats it.
